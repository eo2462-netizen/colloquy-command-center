Week 1, Day 5

Finance Concepts

The six concepts you need to own cold. Read each, then close the page and explain it out loud. If you can do that, you own it.

Concept 01
TTM Revenue Multiples
The dominant valuation method in music catalog transactions. You take the trailing twelve months of net revenue (NPS for publishing, NLS for recorded) and multiply by an industry benchmark. Current benchmarks: 16.1x NPS, 13.0x NLS. The multiple reflects market consensus about asset quality and growth expectations.
Why it matters for your thesisIf an exploitation event floods the TTM window with non-recurring revenue, and a buyer applies 16x to that inflated figure, the valuation may embed earnings that won't repeat. That's the core distortion risk.
Concept 02
Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)
Estimates the present value of all future expected cash flows, discounted by the cost of capital (WACC). Forces the analyst to project forward rather than assume the past repeats. The discount rate for stable catalog songs (18+ months old) is typically ~8.25%; for newer, riskier songs it's 11.75%+ (Kroll/Kobalt). DCF is best practice but harder to execute than multiples.
The tension your thesis exploitsDCF requires an honest view of whether exploitation-driven revenue is durable. Multiples just apply a number to whatever TTM shows. That gap is where your research lives.
Concept 03
Asset-Backed Securitization (ABS)
A financing structure where a company pools its royalty-generating assets into a trust and sells notes backed by those cash flows to institutional investors (pension funds, insurance companies). The trust pays interest from royalties. Music ABS has surged since 2022 — Concord ($1.8B, $850M), Hipgnosis ($1.5B), Kobalt ($267M) are all recent examples. Apollo (Scipio's firm) underwrites and anchors many of these.
The financing loopCatalog value → appraised by Kroll/Duff & Phelps → supports debt issuance at 52–65% LTV → if TTM revenue inflated by exploitation spike → valuation may be overstated → debt may be over-leveraged.
Concept 04
Loan-to-Value Ratio (LTV)
The ratio of debt to the appraised value of the underlying asset. In music ABS, typical LTV is 52–65%. Lower LTV = more conservative (more equity buffer). Lenders get comfortable advancing capital at a target LTV based on third-party valuations. If the appraised value rises (due to exploitation-driven revenue), the company can "upsize" its debt — borrow more against the same catalog.
Scipio's example$100M catalog at 65% LTV = $65M debt. Catalog grows to $150M through exploitation campaigns. New debt capacity = $150M × 65% = $97.5M. Incremental proceeds = $32.5M. That's real money extracted without a sale.
Concept 05
Step Change vs. Spike
Scipio's key framing — and the central analytical question of your thesis. A "step change" means streaming performance permanently elevated to a new, higher baseline after an exploitation event. A "spike" means a temporary surge that eventually reverts to pre-event levels. The distinction determines whether post-event revenue is durable (legitimate valuation input) or distortive (should be normalized).
Case study applicationKate Bush: did streaming stay elevated in 2023–2024? Fleetwood Mac: did "Dreams" produce a new baseline or revert? ABBA: does Voyage represent a durable commercial renaissance? This is the question your empirical work answers.
Concept 06
Normalization
The valuation practice of adjusting historical revenue to remove one-time or non-recurring items before applying multiples or building DCF projections. Alderbrook (2022) specifically recommends normalizing "unusually high sync" income. In practice, many buyers still apply multiples to un-normalized TTM figures — that's the gap your thesis documents and provides evidence to close.
Why practitioners need your researchTo normalize a spike, you need to know how large the spike was and how long it lasted. That's exactly the empirical pattern documentation your case studies provide. Your thesis = the data that makes normalization decisions evidence-based.

Vocab & Cheat Sheet

Flash card-style. Know every term cold. The formulas are ones you should be able to run mentally in a conversation.

The Core Valuation Formula
Catalog Value = TTM Net Revenue × Multiple (16.1x NPS / 13.0x NLS)
Your thesis: if TTM Net Revenue is inflated by a non-recurring exploitation event, the resulting Catalog Value is overstated by exactly (spike revenue × multiple). E.g., $100K spike × 16x = $1.6M valuation distortion.
Scipio's Debt Upsize Formula
New Debt Capacity = New Appraised Value × LTV%
Incremental Proceeds = New Debt Capacity − Old Debt
Example: $100M → $150M catalog. LTV = 65%. Old debt = $65M. New capacity = $97.5M. Incremental = $32.5M. This is how value creation is monetized without a sale.
DCF Simplified
PV = Σ CF(t) / (1 + r)^t
PV = present value. CF(t) = cash flow in year t. r = discount rate (WACC). For stable catalog: r ≈ 8.25%. For new/risky songs: r ≈ 11.75%. Higher risk = higher discount = lower present value. Exploitation spikes that don't persist inflate CF(t) projections when recent TTM is used as the base.

TTM (Trailing Twelve Months)
The last 12 months of revenue used as the base for valuation multiples. Central to your thesis — exploitation events that hit within this window directly affect calculated catalog value.
NPS (Net Publisher's Share)
The net income retained by a music publisher after paying the songwriter's share of royalties. Current benchmark multiple: 16.1x NPS for non-iconic, 17.5x for iconic catalogs.
NLS (Net Label Share)
The net income retained by a record label after artist royalties. Current benchmark: 13.0x NLS for non-iconic, 14.2x for iconic catalogs. Always trades at a discount to NPS due to more concentrated risk.
WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital)
The blended cost of a company's debt and equity financing. Used as the discount rate in DCF models. Lower WACC = higher catalog valuation. ABS financing lowers WACC by about 1.13% vs. bank debt (per Shot Tower), allowing buyers to pay ~10% more for the same asset.
ABS (Asset-Backed Securitization)
Financing structure where royalty-generating catalogs are pooled as collateral for notes sold to institutional investors. Rated by Kroll, Moody's. Music ABS market exceeded $4.4B in 2025.
LTV (Loan-to-Value Ratio)
Debt as a percentage of appraised asset value. Music ABS typically ranges from 52–65% LTV. Covenants require maintaining LTV below a threshold — if value drops, company may need to pay down debt.
Normalization
Adjusting historical revenue figures to remove one-time or non-recurring items. Best practice per Alderbrook (2022) for sync income and exploitation event spikes. Not consistently applied in industry practice — that's your thesis problem.
Step Change
A permanent upward shift in streaming performance following an exploitation event — the new baseline is sustainably higher than pre-event. Durable value creation. Represents legitimate valuation input.
Spike / Reversion
A temporary surge in streaming that eventually returns to pre-event baseline. Creates TTM revenue distortion if it falls within the valuation window. Should be normalized — but often isn't.
Exploitation Event
Any event that generates renewed attention to an existing catalog: sync placement, TikTok virality, biopic, anniversary campaign, artist death, remix. Can be positive (revenue-generating) or negative (reputational, revenue-destroying).
Deep Catalog
Luminate's definition: music streamed 5+ years after release. Rock is the most deep-catalog-heavy genre (72.6% of streams). Classic catalog is specifically valued for predictable, stable royalty income — predictability that exploitation events can temporarily distort.
Kroll Bond Rating Agency (KBRA)
Third-party valuation and ratings agency used to appraise music catalogs and rate ABS notes. Kobalt's catalog appraised at $410M; Concord's at $5B+. Their valuations directly set the ABS debt capacity — which is why accurate TTM revenue inputs matter so much.
Iconic Premium
The valuation premium paid for culturally significant catalogs (Beatles, Springsteen, Dylan). Multiples of 22–30x+ vs. 16x standard. Shot Tower (2025) notes these premiums are justified by perceived durability — which your thesis tests against empirical streaming data.
Securitization vs. Investment Trust
Investment trusts (Hipgnosis Songs Fund, Round Hill) were publicly traded catalog vehicles — now essentially gone. ABS is private, institutional, and has replaced them as the dominant financing vehicle. ABS investors have longer time horizons and don't face "trial in the court of public opinion."
